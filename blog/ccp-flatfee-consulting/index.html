The true cost of CCP: flat fee, success based and traditional consulting

Last updated: April 15, 2026

BizGrants Consulting · 12 December 2025 · 5 min read

The first question most Singapore SMEs ask when scoping a CCP application is “how much does this actually cost?” The answer depends almost entirely on the fee model the advisor uses. Hourly retainers, percentage-of-grant success fees, and flat-fee structures price the same work very differently, and each model creates different incentives for the advisor. This article walks through the three most common structures in the Singapore market, why we use a flat fee with an approval-linked component, and what an SME should look for in a service agreement before they sign.

Comparing CCP consulting models

Model How it works Pros Cons
Traditional hourly or retainer Pay per hour or per month regardless of outcome Deep engagement; may support broader HR or transformation work High upfront cost and no guarantee of CCP approval; harder for SMEs to budget
Pure success fee Pay a % of approved grant amount only if funded No upfront risk; advisor is motivated to secure approval Percentages can be high; some advisors select only “easy” cases
Flat fee with approval linked component (BizGrants) Fixed project fee, with a portion collected only upon approval Transparent and capped cost; risk sharing; suitable for complex cases Works best for employers willing to commit time and documents

Why BizGrants uses a flat fee with an approval-linked component

The hybrid structure exists because each pure model has a failure mode we wanted to avoid. Pure hourly billing creates an incentive to expand scope; pure percentage success fees create an incentive to take only easy cases. A flat fee with an approval-linked component splits the engagement into a base fee that funds the analytical work (eligibility, role redesign, OJT design, submission) and a smaller approval-linked component that aligns the advisor with outcome.

  • Costs are clear from day one, with no surprise charges or rate creep
  • The approval-linked component is collected only when the CCP is approved by Workforce Singapore
  • We can take on technically complex cases, including multi-role cohorts and Job Redesign Reskilling tracks, rather than only low-hanging-fruit hires
  • The model rewards quality of documentation and OJT, not application volume
  • Termination clauses are symmetrical: if we cannot make the case, we say so before scoping fees apply

What an SME should look for in a CCP service agreement

The fee structure matters less than the scope definition. Three contract clauses we recommend every SME read carefully, regardless of which advisor they choose:

  • Scope and deliverables. The agreement should list the artefacts produced (eligibility memo, redesigned JD, OJT plan, submission package, claim support) and the number of clarification rounds covered. Vague scope is the most common cause of fee disputes.
  • Trigger for the success or approval-linked component. “Approval” should be defined precisely: is it the offer letter from Workforce Singapore, the first approved claim, or the full disbursement? Each definition has different cash flow implications.
  • Termination and clawback. If the application is rejected or the candidate leaves before OJT completion, what fees are refundable, what fees are retained, and on what timeline? This clause is the most-skipped and the most-litigated.

What you receive in a CCP advisory project

  • Eligibility review and grant mapping against ACRA, headcount plan, candidate background, and the Workforce Singapore programme catalogue
  • Role redesign and JD drafting with the tools, decisions, and KPIs the assessor expects to see
  • OJT planning aligned to SkillsFuture frameworks where they apply, with named supervisors, artefacts, and assessment moments
  • Application form completion and clarification handling, with a single point of contact for programme partner queries
  • Claims support across the OJT period, including the evidence log structure that protects the eventual disbursement
  • Post-approval advisory on retention, reporting, and how to position a follow-on application from the same employer

For most SMEs, the choice is not really between flat fee and success fee. It is between a transparent scope with predictable cash flow and an opaque scope with cost surprises. A boutique fund manager cohort we ran shows what a multi-role engagement looks like under a flat-fee structure, including how the cap headroom was modelled at scoping rather than at submission.

Common cost mistakes SMEs make

Three patterns we see repeatedly when an SME comes to us after engaging another advisor:

  • Underestimating internal time cost. The advisor’s fee is only one input. The HR lead and the supervisor will spend meaningful time on documents, OJT design reviews, and clarification responses regardless of the fee model. Plan for 20 to 40 hours of internal effort per single-role application, more for multi-role cohorts.
  • Choosing the lowest fee without checking scope. A 30% cheaper engagement that excludes claim support is more expensive than a slightly higher fee that includes it, because the claim work is non-trivial and is exactly when the OJT documentation matters most.
  • Paying upfront for a percentage success fee on a marginal case. If the case is borderline, a percentage fee structure incentivises the advisor to push for submission rather than recommend a redesign first. A written go or no-go memo from a flat-fee engagement protects against this.

How to compare advisors before signing

When evaluating two advisors with different fee structures for the same scope, three diagnostic questions sharpen the decision:

  • Ask each to walk you through one prior engagement that was rejected, and what they recommended next. Advisors that only volunteer success stories are screening their case selection too tightly.
  • Ask for sample artefacts (a redesigned JD, an OJT plan, an evidence log) from a recent application, redacted as needed. The artefact quality is a more reliable signal than the pricing structure.
  • Ask which programme partner they have submitted through most recently, and how their relationship with that partner shapes clarification timing. The honest answer is informative; the marketing answer is also informative.

FAQ on flat fee CCP consulting

  • Q: What if my CCP is not approved?
    A: You do not pay the approval-linked component, and we will provide a written debrief on what can be improved for a future attempt. The base fee covers the analytical work that has been done regardless of outcome.
  • Q: Are there hidden charges?
    A: No. All charges are stated in the service agreement before the engagement starts. Out-of-scope work (additional candidates added mid-application, full claims support beyond the agreed scope) is quoted separately and only proceeds with written approval.
  • Q: How does the flat fee compare to a percentage success fee for a typical SME?
    A: For a single-role application at the cap, a flat-fee model is usually 30 to 50% cheaper than a 15 to 20% success fee on the approved grant amount. For multi-role cohorts, the gap widens further because the fixed fee scales sub-linearly with the number of roles.
  • Q: Can we engage you for just the OJT plan, not the full submission?
    A: Yes. We unbundle the engagement when an SME has internal HR capacity for the submission but wants advisory on the OJT design, which is the most technically demanding part of the application.
  • Q: How do I start?
    A: Book a free eligibility call and we will propose a fee and scope before you commit. The eligibility call is 30 minutes and produces a written go or no-go memo regardless of whether you proceed with us.
